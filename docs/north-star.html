Pherix · north star

Make being wrong cheap.

The essence of Pherix, fixed. Everything we build is judged against this page: does it serve the one promise, and does it fit the four axes? If not, it is drift.

Pherix makes an agent's actions undoable and governed, so you can let it run autonomously — because a mistake is one rollback away, not a catastrophe.

Every other tool lowers the cost of detection — it tells you, after, that you were wrong. Pherix lowers the cost of being wrong. Only the second one actually lets you deploy the agent.

The four questions

Pherix is not a grab-bag of features. It is the complete answer to the only four questions a system needs to answer to make an action transactional — one axis each, each extended independently. That completeness is why the codebase is small: nothing is missing; the problem genuinely factors into four.

Q1 · How do actions reach us?

Interception

Pherix can only undo what it sees, so the agent's actions must go through it, not around it. The point where we plug in to catch them.

Filled by: Python library · TypeScript SDK · MCP gateway · agent-agnostic sandbox. Scope is settled here — Python + TS, nothing more.

Q2 · What do we allow?

Policy

The rules for what the agent may do and when to stop and ask. Not a pile of canned rules — a clean slate the buyer writes their own against, with expressive primitives.

Filled by: allow/deny · caps · the human gate · situation-aware checks · starter templates. The base is built; expressiveness is the depth.

Q3 · How do we undo what can be undone?

Adapters

For each kind of resource, a plug-in that knows how to back it up and undo it. The backend does the real work (savepoints, copy-on-write); we don't reinvent it.

Filled by: SQLite · filesystem · HTTP — and the base to grow: Postgres, MySQL, MongoDB, S3.

Q4 · How do we undo what can't?

Compensators

Some actions can't be reversed (charge a card, send an email). So we don't fire them until the end — and pair each with a vetted undo. The fiddly-to-get-right part, done once and proven.

Filled by: the staged/gated lane + a catalog of paired undos (charge→refund, invite→revoke, provision→delete).

How an action moves through them

The four axes aren't a list — they're a pipeline. One action's path:

agent doesan action
→
Q1caught (interception)
→
Q2allowed? (policy)
→
Q3 · reversiblesnapshot & run (adapter) — undo on failure
Q4 · irreversiblestage → gate → fire at the end (compensator on standby)

…and every step is written to the journal — the append-only log the four axes produce.

Adapters and compensators are two different undo mechanisms, not one. Adapter restore = state rollback: snapshot the resource before, revert to it after — exact, and needs no knowledge of what the action meant. Compensator = semantic inverse: when you can't snapshot the outside world, run an opposite action (charge → refund) — approximate, and needs to know the meaning. Restore does not "use compensation"; compensation is the fallback for exactly when snapshotting is impossible. The dividing line is supports_rollback(). They only meet at the journal — the backward fold calls restore for reversibles and the compensator for irreversibles. Same operation, two mechanisms: that is why they are two axes.

Everything else is one of these four

The axes are not just sufficient — they are exhaustive. Every capability people associate with Pherix collapses into the four plus the journal, rather than adding a fifth thing:

  • substrateAudit — not an axis. The journal is the audit record; the four axes write to it. Audit search & retention are how you package it, not a new mechanism.
  • propertyIsolation (many agents at once) — not an axis. A correctness property of how adapters and the journal behave under concurrency.
  • operationReplay & dry-run — not axes. Operations over the journal the four already produced: re-run it forward, or against a snapshot then discard.
  • deliveryThe control plane — not a mechanism. How the four axes are delivered and sold at organisation scale (policy management, audit search, multi-agent).
  • re-appliedGoverned memory — not a new axis. An agent's memory is just another resource: an adapter + a policy. The existing axes pointed at a new target.
Why the simplicity is earned, not thin. A small codebase worries people who equate size with value. But these four axes completely span the problem of making an action safe — there is no fifth question. The smallness is the signature of a clean factoring, the same reason a good basis is small. We grow by deepening each axis (more adapters, more compensators, richer policy) and by making the hard correctness verifiable — never by padding.

What Pherix is not

The north star also fixes the boundaries. If a proposed feature is one of these, it is out of scope by definition:

Not durable execution

We transact your resources, not your code. Resumable workflows / retry orchestration is Temporal's job — ceded deliberately.

Not observability

We enforce, we don't merely watch. Traces and dashboards are LangSmith / Langfuse. Audit falls out of our journal for free, as a byproduct.

Not an agent framework

We don't run agents, call models, or orchestrate prompts. We wrap the tool-call layer of an agent that already exists.

The value, in one line. We make an agent's actions undoable — snapshotting the reversible ones (adapters) and pairing the irreversible ones with vetted undos (compensators) — all under a policy you write, on a journal that is its own audit trail.

Why the small core is correct, not just small

A simple core worries people who equate size with value. The answer isn't "trust us, it's elegant" — it's a property you can watch: as we grow each axis, the shared substrate (the journal + the per-axis protocols) becomes more general, not more bloated. Extending an axis does one of two things, and both strengthen the core:

  • confirmsA new instance satisfies the existing protocol unchanged — a new adapter implements snapshot/apply/restore with no engine surgery. That is evidence the abstraction was general enough. Three reversibility lanes already proved it; every new adapter confirms it again.
  • generalisesAn extension forces the shared fold to a more general form — and every axis then inherits it. Crash-recovery re-drove the backward fold from the durable journal (promoting the journal from "audit record" to "the thing you rebuild from"); cross-process isolation generalised coordination past the process boundary; world-state policy generalised the policy context to read live state. Each lifted the substrate for all four axes at once.
Convergent generalisation. The failure mode of a wrong abstraction is the opposite — divergent special-casing, where every new resource needs bespoke engine branches and the core accretes exceptions. We don't see that: the MCP gateway landed with core/ essentially untouched, and the hardening pass added capability through the journal, not through per-feature surgery. A core that generalises as it grows is the signature of a correct factoring. That is why small is not thin.

What we're building — the product

The anchor is also concrete. Once the current fleet lands, Pherix is a coherent product, not a pile of parts — and deliberately not a hosted platform yet. Five things, no more; every worktree maps to one of them:

  • engineA deep engine across the four axes. The hardened core (crash-consistent recovery, cross-process isolation, world-state policy, the longitudinal envelope) + breadth: a catalog of adapters (Postgres, MySQL, Mongo, S3, Redis…) and vetted compensators (charge→refund and the common SaaS undos) — tested to its laws (property-based, chaos, differential), not just examples.
  • 2 languagesPython and TypeScript — the two languages agent infra is actually built in. Nothing else. (Governed memory rides the same engine: an adapter + a policy.)
  • landingA simple landing page — codey, one clear promise, the demo front-and-centre. Not a marketing site.
  • demosWorking demos — three scenarios + the OpenClaw air-gapped red-team, genuinely autonomous and filmable: a real agent making a real mistake that Pherix contains.
  • consoleA clean, easy backend console — the inspector (watch the four axes live, read and search the audit trail) + a governance UI to build and preview a policy before it ships. The thing a buyer actually touches.
Explicitly not yet: the hosted, multi-tenant control plane — auth, SSO, retention at scale, billing, compliance certs. That is design-partner-pulled, post-demo. The console above is its seed, run locally.

The roadmap is small now that the value is crystallised: deepen the axes, in two languages, then go to market. No slice graph — the axes are the structure.

  • doneThe hardened engine, the breadth, the console, the landing. The anchor. Build to the base of each axis (the common adapters, common compensators, the expressive policy slate); let the buyer bring the edge cases. Test each axis hard — real behaviour, not ratio-chasing.
