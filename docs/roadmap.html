The one mental model — everything is a traversal of the journal

A Transaction is an ordered, append-only list of Effects — the journal. A ResourceAdapter makes each entry executable and reversible against a real backend (SQL savepoint, filesystem copy, HTTP call). Every capability the roadmap delivers is then just a different way to walk that journal: fold forward, fold backward, fold against a snapshot, or diff it. The eight slices below are not eight subsystems — they are progressively richer traversals of one engine.

Where am I? — between Slice 5 and Slice 6

What's behind you Slices 1–5, merged

The engine is complete in the ACI sense — atomicity, consistency-within-a-txn, isolation — and the journal is now executable. Slice 1 (ad4e9c0) shipped the reversible-path engine. Slice 2 (c926f97) proved the adapter protocol with copy-on-write filesystem snapshots. Slice 3 (a13018b) added the irreversible lane: staging, compensators-as-tools, the gate, partial-commit unwind. Slice 4 (0fab344) brought isolation online with read/write-set capture, per-adapter versioning, the commit-time diff, and three resolution policies. Slice 5 makes the journal executable: a top-level pherix.replay(...) folds the journal forward against operator-supplied fresh adapters — verify asserts each recorded result equals the replayed result (with a per-tool @tool(comparator=fn) escape hatch), reconstruct rebuilds the world from the journal; irreversible APPLIED effects are skip-and-reused, never re-fired. 249 tests green (+15 over Slice 4 baseline).

What's in flight Slice 6 — real policy engine

With the journal now executable, Slice 6 starts treating it as a policy substrate: capability grants, spend caps, content-aware rules — evaluated at stage-time and again at commit-time. The Slice 1 allow-list was a placeholder; the commit-time re-eval hook is already wired in runtime.py (it's a no-op against today's stateless policy). The work is the rule vocabulary, the evaluator, and the test corpus.

01 SQL adapter + engine

done · merged ad4e9c0 69 tests

The reversible path end-to-end. A SQLite SAVEPOINT is what makes rollback correct-by-construction.

Scope & what shipped

Why this slice first. A transaction system without a working reversible adapter is a logging library. SQLite gives us a backend whose semantics already match what we need — SAVEPOINT / ROLLBACK TO SAVEPOINT is a real, atomic snapshot/restore pair. The slice exists to prove the engine on the easy case before the hard ones.

Shipped.

  • Effect dataclass with read_keys / write_keys slots already present (so Slice 4 isolation isn't a retrofit).
  • Transaction + TxnState state machine — OPEN → STAGED → COMMITTED / ROLLED_BACK.
  • ResourceAdapter protocol; SQLiteAdapter implements snapshot/apply/restore.
  • @tool decorator + registry; agent_txn() context manager intercepts via a ContextVar.
  • SQLite append-only audit journal — transaction + effect records.
  • Minimal allow-list policy at stage-time.

Outstanding from review. Four follow-ups (see section below) — none block Slice 1's correctness; they gate later slices.

02 Filesystem adapter (copy-on-write)

done · merged c926f97 103 tests · +34

The protocol stress-test passed: file copies and database savepoints share no implementation but the same interface, and a mixed SQL+FS transaction rolls back through both with no runtime special-cases.

What shipped, decisions, evidence

The point, in retrospect. Slice 2 was the first real test of whether ResourceAdapter was a genuine abstraction or an SQLite-shaped hole. File copies and database savepoints share no implementation; if they share a protocol, the engine survives. The runtime needed no special-case code for FS — the protocol held its shape.

Shipped (decisions, locked then realised).

  • D1 — TransactionalResourceAdapter sub-protocol. A Protocol an adapter opts into by declaring begin/commit/rollback alongside the base snapshot/apply/restore. The runtime swapped hasattr-sniffing for isinstance dispatch — typo'd begin now fails at type-check rather than silently no-op'ing. Closed Slice-1 follow-up #2.
  • D2 — FsHandle: rooted + lazy snapshot. FilesystemAdapter(root: Path); tools receive an FsHandle first arg (same shape as SQL's conn injection). Path-traversal and symlink escape rejected via Path.resolve() + is_relative_to(root) — defence at the handle boundary, not after the write. Snapshots are lazy: backup is taken at first touch of a path inside an effect; subsequent touches in the same effect don't re-snapshot.
  • D3 — mixed-resource demo as the acceptance bar. One agent_txn carrying {"sql": SQLiteAdapter, "fs": FilesystemAdapter}, an agent calling both kinds of tools, rollback wiping both, commit persisting both. Zero runtime changes beyond the D1 swap.

Out of scope (as planned). Staging, gating, compensators, isolation, replay, dry-run, MCP. The read_keys / write_keys slots stayed inert, awaiting Slice 4.

Evidence. pytest -q → 103 passing, +34 over Slice 1. Round-trip restore verified for the file-didn't-exist, file-existed, and file-was-deleted edge cases. Path-traversal / symlink-escape rejected. Backup tempdir cleaned up on every path. Mixed-resource demo passes.

03 HTTP / irreversible adapter

done · merged a13018b 159 tests · +56

Effects that cannot be undone. The runtime stops folding forward live; effects ride in STAGED until commit. Compensators act as semantic left-inverses; un-compensated irreversibles gate.

What shipped · the five decisions, realised

The shift, in retrospect. Slices 1–2 worked because the underlying resources support a true snapshot+restore pair. HTTP doesn't. The runtime stops applying live for non-rollback adapters and stages: every irreversible effect rides in EffectStatus.STAGED, the agent receives a StagedResult(effect_id) sentinel, and the effect's real fire-time moves to commit(). The journal stays the ground truth — staging is a pause in the forward fold.

Locked & shipped.

  • D1 — StagedResult sentinel. Single field, just the effect_id. No semantic payload — agents that branch on it are asking a question outside the model.
  • D2 — Compensators as @tool-registered names. Resolved at fire-time; typos caught at stage-time via CompensatorNotRegistered. compensator ∘ tool ≈ identity is the developer's assertion; Pherix doesn't verify it.
  • D3 — Pre-approval via approve_irreversible(effect_id). Out-of-band verdict, per-transaction. Unknown effect_ids raise rather than silently accepted. Un-compensated irreversibles without a verdict make commit() raise GateBlocked.
  • D4 — TOCTOU-safe policy re-eval at commit. The hook is wired even though Slice 1's allow-list is stateless — the right place for Slice 6's state-dependent policy.
  • D5 — Compensator auto-fire on partial-commit failure. Mid-fire failure walks the journal backward: compensator for fired irreversibles, savepoint-restore for reversibles. Missing/raising compensator → STUCK; everything else → ROLLED_BACK. Operator's recovery target is the irreversible-only journal, which is preserved even in STUCK.

FU#1 closed before the slice. strict_json_default in effects.py now enforces journal-able args at Effect construction; silent str()-coercion is gone.

04 Isolation

done · merged 0fab344 226 tests · +67

The "I" in ACID. Every effect's reads and writes are journalled with versions; at commit, the diff folds the journal against current adapter state and the resolution policy fires.

What shipped · the five decisions, realised

The shift. Slices 1–3 give each single agent atomicity, durability, and credible consistency. Isolation is a relational property between concurrent transactions — two flawless agents can lose updates against each other if their reads and writes interleave. Slice 4 brings the previously-inert read_keys / write_keys slots online.

Locked & shipped.

  • D1 — Automatic capture via the resource handle. FsHandle.read/write/delete records keys into the active Effect automatically; SQL has explicit execute_isolated(conn, stmt, params, reads, writes) because SQL parsing is out of scope. The active effect is bound via a ContextVar set by the runtime around adapter.apply.
  • D2 — Per-adapter versioning. VersionedResourceAdapter sub-protocol with read_version / write_version. SQL: a logical counter side-table (_pherix_versions) bumped atomically by UPSERT…RETURNING; a separate meta-connection answers read_version queries so cross-process commits see each other's bumps (the main connection's BEGIN snapshot otherwise hides them). FS: sha256 content hash, with a "__missing__" sentinel for absent paths. HTTPAdapter doesn't conform — irreversibles are isolated-by-construction via staging.
  • D3 — Commit-time diff only. For each (resource, key, v_at_read), ask the adapter for read_version now — if it moved, conflict. Per-effect checking would need a global lock; commit-time is the only point where the world can have moved on us. The check_conflicts fold filters self-bumps (the same txn reading then writing a key would otherwise mis-flag its own monotonic-counter bump).
  • D4 — Resolution as a callable f: Conflict → Action. Three substrates: Abort() (default — raise IsolationConflict and unwind), Retry(N) (raise an internal signal that run_txn(fn, ...) catches to replay the body up to N times), Serialize() (block on a per-(resource, key) event from the in-process JournalRegistry until concurrent writers close, then re-check). Retry with the with agent_txn(...) form degrades to Abort — a context-manager body is not a callable Pherix owns.
  • D5 — Single shared journal on a single host. Two arbitration substrates ship: the in-process JournalRegistry singleton (multi-agent in one Python process), and the filesystem-shared SQLite side-table (multi-process, single host — verified by a subprocess-based integration test). Cross-host arbitration is the Slice 8 gateway's job, not Raft/Paxos.

Demo. examples/slice4_demo.py — two fake agents race on a shared bank-account row; each policy produces a different, serializability-preserving outcome.

05 Replay from the journal

done · merged249 tests · +15

The journal is now executable. pherix.replay(txn_id, adapters, source_audit=...) folds it forward against fresh state; verify asserts results match, reconstruct accepts today's results as the rebuilt world.

What shipped · the five decisions, realised

The shift, in retrospect. Slices 1–4 made the journal canonical; Slice 5 makes it executable. The "nearly free" framing held — replay is the existing commit path's forward fold, pointed at operator-supplied fresh adapters, with per-effect equality at each step (verify) or accept-as-new-state (reconstruct). No new state machine, no new abstractions in effects.py / transaction.py / audit.py beyond a single Transaction.replayed_from field and one new audit column.

Locked & shipped.

  • D1 — Verify and reconstruct share the engine. One walker; the mode parameter selects what to do with each effect's result.
  • D2 — JSON-by-default equality with per-tool escape hatch. Default comparator canonicalises both sides through strict_json_default and string-equates. @tool(comparator=fn) overrides for tools whose results vary legitimately (timestamps, generated IDs).
  • D3 — Already-applied irreversibles skip-and-reuse. Source status=APPLIED + reversible=False → never re-fired; the journal is the witness. This retired the Slice-3 follow-up that flagged the idempotency test as "a pin, not a scenario."
  • D4 — Mode parameter from D1 carries non-determinism behaviour. Under reconstruct, today's result becomes the new world's state; under verify, the per-tool comparator decides whether a varying result is tolerated.
  • D5 — New top-level replay(), not a run_txn extension. The replay txn carries replayed_from = source_txn_id; its own journal lands in target_audit (default in-memory). Operator hands in the fresh adapters; Pherix doesn't try to construct them.

Demo. examples/slice5_demo.py — replays a mixed SQL+FS+HTTP source journal three ways: verify against fresh state; reconstruct on yet-another fresh substrate; tamper the audit row, watch verify diverge.

Slice-3 follow-up #3 retired. Irreversible-APPLIED skip-and-reuse is now exercised by the real replay walker, not a status-flip pin.

06 Real policy engine

next on path

Capability grants, spend caps, content-aware rules — evaluated at stage-time AND again at commit-time (TOCTOU safety).

Why evaluate twice

Between stage-time and commit-time, state can change. A budget that was within cap when an effect staged can exceed cap by the time the transaction commits (because another effect in the same transaction has since spent). Single-shot policy is a time-of-check / time-of-use bug. Pherix evaluates at both edges — stage to fail fast and give the agent useful feedback; commit to actually enforce.

Slice 1 ships an allow-list as a placeholder. Slice 6 makes it real: declarative rules over the journal at commit-time.

07 Speculative dry-run

future

Fold the journal forward against a snapshot, then discard the snapshot. Same engine; different terminator.

What an agent gets out of this

Agents that can preview the consequences of their tool calls — full state diff, policy result, irreversible-effect list — before any of it is real. The implementation is the commit fold, but pointed at an ephemeral copy of state, with the diff returned as the result. Like Slice 5, this is "nearly free once the journal exists."

08 MCP gateway front-end

future

The same core engine, driven by an MCP proxy instead of a Python import. Validates the frontends/ discipline.

Why it's the last slice, not the first

Building Pherix as a library first forces the core to be front-end-agnostic. The MCP proxy is a different driver for the same engine — if the architectural discipline in core/ vs frontends/ has held, this slice is small (a process boundary, a wire format) and adds no new semantics. If it requires changes in core/, the discipline failed somewhere earlier and we'd rather find out now than at v1.0.

Beyond Slice 8 — the trilogy

SPECULATIVE

By the end of Slice 8 the engine is complete — snapshot/rollback (Slices 1–3), isolation (Slice 4), replay (Slice 5), policy (Slice 6), dry-run (Slice 7), and a language-agnostic gateway (Slice 8). Past that line sit two parallel pillars over the same substrate. They aren't sequels to the determinism pillar; they're different traversals of the same journal.

Determinism + Memory + Audit

All three derived from the same substrate. Walk the journal forward / backward = determinism. Index the journal = memory. Query the journal = audit. One substrate, three faces.

S2

Bootstrap milestone — Slice 2 already makes one face real free with Slice 2

With FilesystemAdapter merged (c926f97), Claude Code's own memory directory is already a Pherix-transactable resource. Saving a memory inside an agent_txn and rolling back when the conversation goes sideways is the first honest dogfood of the memory face — no new adapter required.

And because every staged effect is journalled before it fires, the audit face is similarly free: the journal we already persist is the audit log. Beyond Slice 8 isn't a hand-wave — both pillars are partially observable in the engine today.

M Memory substrate

pillar · beyond Slice 8 depends on 3 · 4 · 6 · 8

Index the journal. VectorAdapter family + memory-specific policy vocabulary + agent-memory frontend. The journal is the ground truth; embeddings are lossy indices over it.

The three components & why each upstream slice matters

What. A complete memory layer over the journal, in three components:

  • VectorAdapter family. Adapters for the data shapes agent memory actually uses — vector stores (HNSW-style indices where "write" rebalances the graph and "read" is a top-k similarity query), KV stores with time-to-live and decay, summarisation-aware stores where the history of a value matters as much as its current contents. Each needs snapshot/apply/restore respecting the backend's real semantics — savepoints don't help when the structure is an approximate-nearest-neighbour index.
  • Memory policy vocabulary. A library of predicates over memory effects: PII classifiers, temporal decay rules ("decay PII after 30 days; never decay user-stated preferences"), scope rules over multi-agent visibility, consensus rules ("this memory commits only if N agents agree"). Each is a function the Slice 6 engine can call at stage-time and commit-time.
  • Agent-memory frontend. The same architectural move as Slice 8's MCP gateway, pointed at agent frameworks — LangChain, Letta, LangMem, Claude Code itself. The framework keeps its API; Pherix provides the transactional, journalled, isolated, policy-gated guarantees underneath.

Why these upstream slices.

  • Slice 3 — memory stores are mostly remote and mostly approximate; they live in the irreversible regime. Staging, compensators, idempotency keys are the machinery memory adapters reuse.
  • Slice 4 — agent memory is concurrent by construction (multi-agent reads, asynchronous writes). Without read/write-set tracking, shared memory is a race condition.
  • Slice 6 — without the policy engine, there is nowhere to plug PII/decay/scope predicates in. Generic capability grants aren't enough to govern shared memory.
  • Slice 8 — the front-end-agnostic discipline has to have held before claiming a memory frontend on top of the same core.

The framing. The journal records every memory effect — every save, every recall, every decay. An embedding index is then just a fold over the journal into approximate-nearest-neighbour space. If the embedding is lost, it can be rebuilt by re-folding. The journal is ground truth; the index is a lossy observable.

A Observability / audit

pillar · beyond Slice 8 depends on 1 · 5 · 6

Query the journal. The journal IS the audit log — this pillar exposes it. A query interface, transaction replay/inspection, compliance views, regulator-friendly exports.

What this is, and what it isn't

What. The audit pillar surfaces the journal as a queryable, inspectable, exportable object. Three components:

  • Query surface. Predicates over the effect journal — by transaction, by tool, by resource, by time window, by policy verdict. The journal already has the structure; this is the layer that exposes it as a first-class API rather than a SQLite file you have to know about.
  • Replay / inspection. Step a committed transaction effect-by-effect against a forked copy of state; diff the state before and after each effect; see exactly which predicate held and which didn't. Slice 5 already builds replay as a fold-forward; this pillar wraps it in an operator-facing surface.
  • Compliance views & exports. Regulator-friendly projections of the journal — who did what, when, against which resource, under which policy verdict, with which approval. Append-only, immutable, signed. The artefact a compliance officer or external auditor can ingest without needing to understand Pherix internals.

What this is NOT. This pillar does not watch LLM traces, score model outputs, or compete with LangSmith / Langfuse / Arize at the observability-of-the-model layer. Pherix sits at the tool-call layer and audits effects on the world. AI-signal-based predicates (toxicity scores, alignment classifiers, jailbreak detectors) plug into Slice 6's policy engine as score-based predicate hooks — they are consumed as one of many predicate types, not produced as a Pherix slice direction. Pherix wraps tool calls; it doesn't run LLMs.

Why these upstream slices.

  • Slice 1 — the SQLite audit journal that already exists is the substrate. The journal is the product; this pillar is the surface.
  • Slice 5 — replay is the operator's primary inspection tool. "Show me exactly what this transaction did, step by step" reduces to fold-forward-against-fresh-state.
  • Slice 6 — every audit row carries the policy verdict that held over it. Without a real policy engine, there's nothing meaningful to audit — every effect is just "allowed because nothing said no."

The framing. Determinism and audit fall out of the same property of the journal: it's an append-only, totally-ordered record of effects with adapter-resolved before/after states. Determinism is the journal walked forward to enforce; audit is the same journal walked forward to explain.

Reading the map

The slices look like a ladder of features. They aren't. They're a ladder of journal traversals applied to progressively harder backends. Slice 1 proved the engine on a backend that already does what we need. Slice 2 proves the protocol holds when the backend doesn't share an implementation. Slices 3–4 add the two structural complications real systems have (irreversibility, concurrency). Slices 5–7 are nearly-free dividends from owning the journal. Slice 8 validates the front-end-agnostic discipline.

Every follow-up dot on the timeline is a Slice-1 simplification whose interest-rate becomes due at a specific later slice. Resolving them on time is how Pherix stays the shape it was designed to be.

The Beyond Slice 8 zone is what the eight-slice engine enables, not what it builds. Two parallel pillars over the same journal: memory (index it) and audit (query it), each a different traversal of the substrate the determinism pillar already owns. The first dogfood of both lands as a free byproduct of Slice 2 — the engine and its descendants meet sooner than the slice numbering suggests.
